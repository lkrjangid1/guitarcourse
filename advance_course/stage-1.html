Stage 01 · Foundation

The Key.

Before you can play a song by ear, you need to know which "neighbourhood" it lives in. That neighbourhood is the key. Find the key, and you've gone from 12 possible chords to 6. Find the key in seconds — by ear — and you have the foundation everything else builds on.

0Why this matters

Imagine you walk into a friend's birthday and someone hands you a guitar. They start singing Tum Hi Ho. What do you do?

The "old way" answer: pull out your phone, search the chord sheet on Ultimate Guitar, try to read it while playing, fall behind, give up.

The "by ear" answer:

  1. Listen for two seconds. What key is this in?
  2. Once you know the key, you have ~6 chords available.
  3. Your first chord is almost always the home chord of that key.
  4. From there, your ear and the song's melody tell you when to change.

That whole sequence takes maybe 8 seconds. Today we learn step 1 — the most important step — by mastering one idea:

"Every song has a home. The home is the key. Find the home, and the rest of the map opens up."

1What is a key, really?

A key is a song's home note (also called the tonic) plus a flavour (major or minor). That's it.

If a song is in G major:

  • The home note is G (सा, if you're using sargam — your tonic).
  • The flavour is major (bright, happy).
  • The home chord — the one the song most wants to "rest on" — is G.
  • Only 7 notes are "in" this key. The other 5 are "outside notes" used sparingly.
  • From those 7 notes, exactly 6 useful chords can be built — and these 6 do 95% of the work in 95% of songs.

That's the entire promise of "finding the key." You shrink your problem from infinite possibilities to a fixed family of 6.

The key analogy

Think of a key like a city. The home note is your house. The 6 diatonic chords are your neighbourhood — places you can walk to easily. The other 5 chromatic notes are the rest of the world — you can visit, but it's a trip. Most songs never leave the neighbourhood.

2The home note test

Here's the practical method to find a song's key. You'll get good at this in about a week of doing it consciously.

1
Listen to the song. Play 30 seconds. Don't worry about words or melody — just feel the music.
2
Hum along. Hum any random note while the song plays. Then slide your hum up or down until it feels locked in — like it belongs.
3
Stop the song. Keep humming. The note you're holding is almost always either the home note (tonic) or its perfect 5th. Eight times out of ten, it's the tonic.
4
Find it on your guitar. Pluck the open strings and the basic frets until one of them matches your hum. That string/fret is your tonic.
5
Test the chord. Play the major chord of that note (e.g., if your hum matched G, play G). Sing along — does it feel "at home"? If yes, you're in that major. If it feels off, try the minor instead — you're probably in the relative minor.

That's the whole process. The trick is doing step 2 — finding the locked-in hum — quickly. Let's drill it.

3The "first and last chord" trick

Before you trust your ear completely, there's a sneaky shortcut that works ~85% of the time:

The shortcut

The first chord of a song is almost always the home chord. The last chord almost certainly is.

So when you hear a song and you have access to any reference — your ear, an Instagram clip, a 5-second YouTube snippet — listen for the very first chord (or the very last one before the song ends). That's your key.

Why this works

Songs are built to feel "complete." The most natural way to start a song is on its tonic (home). The most natural way to end is on its tonic. Composers do this without thinking because anything else feels unresolved — like leaving the front door open when you go to bed.

Examples from songs you know

Tum Hi Ho — Aashiqui 2
Arijit Singh · 2013

First chord: Em. Last chord: Em. The song lives in E minor.

What this tells you: the home is Em. The chord family you can use is the E minor family — Em, Am, B7 (or Bm), C, D, G. Suddenly you're not guessing 12 chords; you're picking from 6.
Kal Ho Na Ho — title track
Sonu Nigam · 2003

First chord: G. Last chord: G. The song lives in G major.

What this tells you: home is G. Your family is G, C, D, Em, Am, Bm. And — sure enough — the entire song uses exactly those.
Channa Mereya
Arijit Singh · ADHM, 2016

First chord: G. Verse moves through G, Em, C, D. Last chord: G. Lives in G major (but it leans toward the Em/minor flavour, which is why it feels nostalgic).

What this tells you: in G major, the vi chord (Em) is the relative minor. Songs that feel "sad in a happy key" usually lean on the vi. We'll explore this in Stage 2.
Where the shortcut fails

About 15% of songs start on a chord that isn't the tonic — usually IV or V. They do this deliberately to create a feeling of "starting in motion." If the first chord doesn't feel like home, check the last chord. If that doesn't feel like home either, the song probably modulates (changes key) — rare in pop, common in Bollywood interludes.

4Major vs minor — the flavour test

Once you've found the home note, the second question is: is the song major (bright) or minor (sad)?

This is easier than it sounds. There's a simple test:

The flavour test

Play the home note. Sing the home note. Then sing the 3rd note above it.

  • If the 3rd feels 4 frets above the home (a major third) → it's major. Bright, happy, resolved.
  • If the 3rd feels 3 frets above (a minor third) → it's minor. Sad, soulful, unresolved.

Quick reference: which 3rd sounds like which mood

Home (tonic)Major 3rd (4 frets up)Minor 3rd (3 frets up)
GB → makes G MajorB♭ → makes Gm
CE → makes C MajorE♭ → makes Cm
DF♯ → makes D MajorF → makes Dm
AC♯ → makes A MajorC → makes Am
EG♯ → makes E MajorG → makes Em

5Mapping melody to key — the singer's shortcut

Here's a singer-specific trick that's pure gold:

When you're singing a song you know but don't have chords for, your voice almost always lands on the home note at the end of a phrase. The end of the last line of the verse. The end of the chorus. The held-out final note. That note is your tonic.

Try this right now

  1. Sing the chorus of Tum Hi Ho in your head. Sing it out loud if you can.
  2. Notice where you naturally land on "...tum hi ho."
  3. That landing note — the "ho" — is the tonic of the song.
  4. Find it on your guitar. Play that note's major or minor chord depending on the mood.
  5. You just identified the key by singing.

This is the easiest, fastest, most reliable method when you don't have a recording handy. Your voice already knows the song — it'll find the home note for you.

Practice this daily

Pick any song you know by heart. Sing it. Catch where your voice rests at phrase endings. Find that note on your guitar. Try its major and minor chord. Done — you've found the key, no chord sheet required.

6The six common keys for guitar

You don't need to know all 24 major and minor keys. On guitar, about six keys account for 90% of pop, folk, and Bollywood music — because these are the keys that lay out comfortably under your fingers.

Here are the six. Memorise these and you can probably play along with any pop song you'll ever hear:

G major

Open-string friendly. The "default" guitar key.

G Am Bm C D Em

Songs in G: Kal Ho Na Ho · Channa Mereya · Raabta · Tujhe Kitna Chahne Lage · countless others

D major

Bright. Common in folk and country.

D Em F♯m G A Bm

Songs in D: Kesariya · Summer of '69 · Wonderful Tonight · many devotional songs

C major

No sharps or flats. Neutral, balanced.

C Dm Em F G Am

Songs in C: Let It Be · Imagine · many ghazals

E minor

The "sad cousin" of G major. Most expressive minor key for guitarists.

Em F♯° G Am Bm C D

Songs in Em: Tum Hi Ho · Galliyan · Kabira · most ballads in their original key

A minor

The "sad cousin" of C major. Pure, classic minor.

Am B° C Dm Em F G

Songs in Am: Agar Tum Saath Ho · Stairway to Heaven · countless ghazals

D minor

The "saddest of all keys" (Spinal Tap was right). Common in old Bollywood.

Dm E° F Gm Am B♭ C

Songs in Dm: Mere Naina Sawan Bhadon · many Kishore-da classics

Capo trick

If a song is in a "hard" key like B♭ or F♯m, you don't actually need to know that key. Put a capo on the right fret and play familiar G/C/D/Em shapes. For example: capo 3 with Em shapes = G minor. Capo 5 with G major shapes = C major. We'll cover this fully in Stage 4 — for now, just know it's an option.

7Real-world drill: identify the key of 5 songs

This is the homework for Stage 1. Do this every day for a week.

Your week-1 practice

  • Pick 5 songs you know well. Random ones — don't think about it.
  • For each song, hum the chorus. Find where your voice lands at the end of phrases.
  • Match that landing note to a string/fret on your guitar.
  • Try playing the major chord first. If it feels right, the song is in that major key.
  • If major feels wrong, try the minor. If that feels right, the song is in the minor.
  • Write down your guess. Check by looking up the chord sheet afterwards.
  • Aim for 70% correct in the first week. By week two, you'll be at 90%+.

Sample worked example: Hawayein (Jab Harry Met Sejal)

Hawayein — Arijit Singh
Pritam · 2017

Step 1: Hum the chorus. "Hawayein... bata rahi hain..."

Step 2: Where does your voice land at the end of "tera pata"? Sing it out — feel the resolution.

Step 3: That landing note is the song's tonic. On guitar, it matches the open D string (with capo on fret 4 it sits at F♯).

Step 4: Try playing D major while singing. Feels right? Yes — bright, optimistic.

Step 5: Song is in D major. (Without capo. With capo 4, you play D shapes but it sounds like F♯ major — the original recording's actual key.)

What you've gained: you didn't look up a single chord sheet, and you already know the song uses some combination of D, Em, F♯m, G, A, Bm. That's enough to start playing along immediately.

8The five most common mistakes at this stage

Mistake 1

Confusing the tonic with the perfect 5th. When you hum along, sometimes your voice locks onto the 5th instead of the tonic — they both sound "stable." Test: try singing down from your hum to a lower note that feels even more "settled." If you find one, that lower note is probably the real tonic.

Mistake 2

Mistaking the relative minor for the home key. G major and E minor share the same notes. So if a song uses G, Em, C, D, you might say "it's in G" when it's actually "in Em." Test: play the song and end on G. Does it feel resolved? If yes, it's G major. If it still feels like wanting more, end on Em — if that resolves, it's E minor.

Mistake 3

Trying to find the key while the song is still playing. Stop the song. Sit in silence. Then sing what you remember. The music's spectrum confuses your ear when you're trying to isolate one note.

Mistake 4

Not singing out loud. Humming in your head doesn't work nearly as well. Your voice and ear are connected in a feedback loop — humming aloud uses both.

Mistake 5

Skipping the verification step. Always test your guess by playing the major (or minor) chord of your guessed tonic while singing the song. If it feels at home, you're right. If it clashes, your tonic is wrong — try a different note.

9What you should be able to do before Stage 2

Stage 1 mastery checklist

  • Hum a song you know and identify the note your voice lands on at phrase endings.
  • Match that note to a string or fret on your guitar without much hunting.
  • Play the major chord of that note and tell whether it "feels at home" with the song.
  • If major feels wrong, switch to minor — and verify it sounds right.
  • Tell, by ear, whether a song is in a major key or a minor key with 80%+ accuracy.
  • Name the six diatonic chords of G major, D major, C major, Em, Am, Dm without thinking.
  • Identify the key of any of the 50+ songs in your Arijit/Kishore songbook by humming the chorus.

If you can check off all of these, you're ready for Stage 2. Don't move on without them — Stage 2 builds directly on these skills, and skipping ahead just makes you re-learn this later.

10A preview of Stage 2

Now that you can identify a song's key, the next question is: which chord do I play, and when?

The answer involves understanding the family of six chords that lives in every key — and learning the personality of each family member. Some chords are "stable" (you can sit on them). Some are "transitional" (they want to move somewhere). Some are "tense" (they need to resolve).

In Stage 2 you'll learn Roman numerals (I, IV, V, vi etc.) — not because they're fancy, but because they let you talk about chord progressions in a way that works in any key. The same I–V–vi–IV progression that powers Tum Hi Ho also powers Let It Be, With or Without You, and thousands of other songs.

For now: do the drills, identify keys for one week, and let your ear catch up. See you in Stage 2.
