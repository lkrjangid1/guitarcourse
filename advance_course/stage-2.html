Stage 02 · The chord family

The Six Friends.

Every key has exactly six useful chords. They're not random — each has a specific personality and a specific role. Once you meet the family, you stop "trying chords until something works" and start knowing what's available.

0What you'll learn

By the end of this stage, you'll be able to:

  • Name the six diatonic chords of any major or minor key, without thinking.
  • Refer to chords by their Roman numeral (I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi) instead of their letter — which lets you talk about progressions in any key.
  • Hear a chord change and know which chord it probably is, based on its role in the family.
  • Use the tension–resolution map to predict where a song is going next.
Why Roman numerals matter

When you understand a song as I–V–vi–IV instead of G–D–Em–C, two huge things happen: (1) you can play the same song in any key just by changing the home chord, and (2) you start recognising that the same progression appears in thousands of songs. Roman numerals are the universal language of how chords move.

1Where the six chords come from

Stage 1 told you that a key has 7 notes (the scale). Take any of those 7 notes and stack two more notes on top — both also from the scale — and you get a chord. That's it. Seven scale notes, seven possible chords.

But the 7th chord is "diminished" (sounds tense, rarely used in pop), so practically speaking, you get six useful chords.

The recipe (using G major as example)

G major's scale: G   A   B   C   D   E   F♯

Build chords by stacking "every other note" — skip one, take one. Like this:

Scale degree:     1     2     3     4     5     6     7
Note in G:        G     A     B     C     D     E     F♯
                  ↓     ↓     ↓     ↓     ↓     ↓     ↓
Build chord:    G-B-D A-C-E B-D-F♯ C-E-G D-F♯-A E-G-B F♯-A-C
Chord name:      G    Am    Bm    C     D     Em    F♯°
Roman numeral:   I    ii    iii   IV    V     vi    vii°
Quality:        major minor minor major major minor diminished

Notice the pattern of qualities — major, minor, minor, major, major, minor, diminished — that's the same for every major key. So if you know the home note, you know the qualities automatically.

The shortcut to remember

"I, IV, V are the three majors.
ii, iii, vi are the three minors.
Capital Roman numeral = major. Lowercase = minor."

Practical use right now

Pick any song in your songbook. Look at its chord sheet. Identify the key (from Stage 1). Then assign Roman numerals to every chord. You'll start seeing patterns immediately — most songs use only I, IV, V, and vi in some order.

3The personality of each chord

This is where the magic begins. Each Roman numeral has a job description. Memorise the personalities and you can predict, from a single chord change, what the song is doing and what's likely to come next.

I
The home · always stable
"Where the song lives"

The tonic. Most songs start here and definitely end here. When you hear I, you feel arrival. Every other chord in the family exists to either leave I and come back, or to delay returning to I.

First chord of 90% of songs Last chord of 99% In G: G
ii
The set-up · gentle motion
"The polite cousin"

A minor chord that almost always leads to V. The combo ii–V–I is the most common cadence in jazz and a huge part of pop. ii sounds wistful but not heavy — like packing your bags.

Usually followed by V Wistful, not sad In G: Am
iii
The drifter · contemplative
"The quiet one"

The least-used family member. Used sparingly, often as a passing chord between I and IV (I → iii → IV). Has a contemplative, slightly mysterious feel. When you hear it, the song is taking a moment to think.

Often passing between I and IV Contemplative In G: Bm
IV
The away · "going somewhere"
"The escape"

The first major chord you typically move to from home. IV feels like opening a door — leaving the living room to explore the rest of the house. Often pairs with V to create the "going-and-returning" effect. I → IV is the most common chord change in popular music, full stop.

"Doorway opening" feel Pairs with V In G: C
V
The pull · "tension that wants to go home"
"The magnet"

This is the most important chord after I. V creates tension that desperately wants to resolve back to I. Every song uses this push-pull. When you hear V, you can almost predict the next chord is I.

Strongest pull home Adds tension In G: D
vi
The cousin · "sad version of home"
"The bittersweet"

The relative minor of I. Shares almost all its notes with I but feels sad. Songs use vi when they want to sound like home but with a melancholic twist. Channa Mereya's nostalgic feel comes from how much it dwells on vi (Em in G major).

"Sad home" Most-used minor In G: Em

Reading this map gives you predictive power:

  • When you hear V, the next chord is probably I (it wants to resolve).
  • When you hear ii, the next chord is probably V (which then resolves to I — the classic ii–V–I).
  • When you hear IV, the song could go anywhere — but usually V or I.
  • When you hear vi, the song is often setting up a IV or V next.

5The four universal progressions

Four chord patterns power roughly 80% of all popular songs ever written. If you know these four, you can play along with thousands of songs without ever opening a chord sheet.

Pattern 1 · The Axis

I – V – vi – IV

The most common progression in modern pop. "The four-chord song" you've seen in viral mashups uses this. Sounds optimistic, anthemic, complete.

7Worked example: Tum Hi Ho through the family lens

Let's put everything together. Tum Hi Ho is in E minor (you found that in Stage 1). The chorus goes:

C – G – D – Em
"Kyunki tum hi ho, ab tum hi ho..."

Let's translate to Roman numerals in E minor. The family is:

  • i = Em
  • III = G (this is the relative major)
  • iv = Am
  • v / V = Bm / B
  • VI = C
  • VII = D

So the chorus is: VI – III – VII – i.

That's the "sad axis" pattern — equivalent to IV – I – V – vi if we thought of it relative to G major instead of E minor (they share the same notes). Either way:

What this analysis tells you
  • This progression is extremely common — you've heard it in hundreds of songs.
  • The chord change D → Em at the end is V → i (in E minor) — the strongest possible resolution.
  • If you transpose the song to a different key, just keep the Roman numerals the same and substitute the right chords for that key. The "feel" stays identical.

9How to practise the family this week

Stage 2 daily practice

  • Pick a key (rotate between G, D, C, Em, Am, Dm — one each day).
  • Play the I chord. Then IV. Then V. Then back to I. Listen to how each one feels.
  • Now play I – vi – IV – V in that key. Then I – V – vi – IV. Then vi – IV – I – V.
  • Pick one song from your songbook. Without looking at the chord sheet, identify the key and play I, IV, V, vi of that key. Try to play along with the song.
  • Sing the chord names out loud as you play them: "G, C, D, G..." — this links the sound and the name in your head.
  • Then sing the Roman numerals as you play: "One, four, five, one..." — this links sound to function.

10Mastery checklist for Stage 2

Ready for Stage 3 when you can:

  • Name all six chords of G, D, C, Em, Am, Dm without looking.
  • Translate between Roman numerals and chord names in any of those keys, in either direction.
  • Tell from a single chord change whether you just heard I→IV, I→V, I→vi, or IV→V.
  • Recognise the four universal progressions when played in any key, ~70% of the time.
  • Play any of the four progressions from memory in G, D, and Em.
  • Look at a chord sheet and immediately translate to Roman numerals.

11Preview of Stage 3

So far you've learned to find a song's key (Stage 1) and what chords are available in that key (Stage 2). What you haven't learned yet is the killer skill — how to know which chord to play, when, while singing your own melody.

The trick: every note you sing belongs to one or more chords in the key. If you can identify which scale degree your voice is on, you can pick the chord that contains that degree — and it will sound right, every time.

In Stage 3 (The Melody Bridge), you'll learn the relationship between melody notes and chord choices. By the end of that stage, you'll be able to sit at a guitar, sing any melody, and accompany yourself without anyone teaching you the song. That's the moment most guitarists describe as "magic" — and it's just one more stage away.
