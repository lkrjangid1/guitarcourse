Stage 03 · Melody to chord

The Melody Bridge.

The single most useful skill in this course. You'll learn to hear (or sing) any melody note, instantly identify which scale degree it is, and pick the chord that contains it. This is how you accompany yourself singing a song no one taught you.

0The big idea

Here's the secret nobody tells beginners: melody and chords are not separate things. A chord is just three notes from the scale stacked up. A melody is one note from the scale at a time. So any given moment, your melody note belongs to one or more of the six chords in the key.

If you can answer "which scale degree is my voice singing right now?" — you can pick a chord that contains that degree, and it will sound right.

"Every melody note tells you which chord it lives in. You just have to listen."
What this stage requires

Stage 1 (key identification) and Stage 2 (chord families and Roman numerals) need to be fairly fluent before this clicks. If you can't yet name the six chords of G major in two seconds, go back and drill Stage 2 a few more days before continuing.

The killer insight

Notice that degrees 1, 3, and 5 are the safest because they live in the most chords. Degrees 4 and 7 are the most "pointed" — they nail down specific chords (IV and V respectively). When your melody hits a 4 or 7, the chord choice is almost predetermined.

2How to find your scale degree in real time

The fundamental skill: while singing, know which number you're on. This is the "ear-training muscle" that takes the longest to build — but pays off forever.

The method

  1. Play the I chord on your guitar and sing along.
  2. Sing scale-degree numbers out loud: "ONE, two, three, four, five, six, seven, ONE."
  3. Cycle through them ascending and descending.
  4. Now play I again and sing just the 1. Then jump randomly to other degrees while saying their numbers: "1... 3... 5... 1... 7... 2... 1...".
  5. The aim is to feel what degree 5 sounds like vs degree 3 vs degree 4 — they each have a distinct flavour.

3The melody-to-chord matrix

This is the most important table in the entire course. Print it. Stick it on your wall. The columns are scale degrees (which note you're singing). The rows are chords (which chord you might play). A ★ means "this is the most natural fit." A ✓ means "works fine." Empty means "avoid — it'll clash."

Chord ↓ / Melody note → 1234567
I ★ ★ ★
ii ★ ★ ★
iii ★ ★ ★
IV ✓ ★ ★
V ✓ ★ ★
vi ★ ★ ★

How to read this:

  • When your voice sings note 1 (G, in G major), the most natural chord is I or vi. Either works.
  • When your voice sings note 4 (C), the natural chord is IV or ii.
  • When your voice sings note 7 (F♯), the natural chord is almost always V.
The 80/20 rule

You don't need to memorise the whole matrix. Just remember: degree 1, 3, or 5 → play I. Degree 4 or 6 → play IV. Degree 2, 5, or 7 → play V. Degree 6 with sad mood → play vi. That covers most situations.

4Worked example: building chords from Tum Hi Ho

Let's reverse-engineer the chorus melody and pick chords from scratch.

Chorus melody (E minor key)
Note: in E minor, the 1 is E. Note degrees here are relative to E minor.
"Kyun - ki tum hi ho" deg: 3 3 5 3 1

Analysis:

  • "Kyunki" lands on degree 3 (G note). Best chord: III (which is G major in E minor). Try playing G — feels great.
  • "Tum" jumps to degree 5 (B note). Best chord: still III or i. Both work.
  • "Hi ho" resolves to degree 1 (E note). Best chord: i. The song "lands home" — that's why it feels conclusive.

So the chord progression under this melody:

G   →   G   →   Em

That's exactly what the song does. You just derived the chord progression by listening to the melody and using the matrix.

5The 4-step live method

Here's how to use everything you've learned in real time, while sitting at a guitar with a song to figure out.

1

Find the key (Stage 1 skills)

Hum the chorus. Where does your voice land at the end of phrases? That note is the tonic. Test major vs minor. Now you know "key of X" and have 6 chord choices instead of 12.

2

Start with I and let your ear lead

Play the I chord. Sing the song's opening line over it. Does it sound right? Most songs start on I, so this works ~85% of the time. If it clashes, try IV or vi — start there instead.

3

Listen for the "wants to change" moment

As you sing, you'll feel a specific spot where the I chord stops feeling right — your voice has moved to a note that clashes. That's your cue. Stop singing for half a second, listen to the melody note you're on, and consult the matrix: which chord contains that note? Switch to it.

4

Use the gravity rules

If you're on V, you can almost always go back to I next. If you're on IV, going to V (and then I) is natural. If you've been on I a while, IV is the easiest move. Trust these gravity rules and you'll rarely pick a wrong next chord.

6Phrase endings — the chord-change signposts

Here's a practical trick: most songs change chords between phrases, not in the middle. So instead of trying to track chord changes note-by-note, listen for where the singer takes a breath. The chord likely changes right there.

Example from Channa Mereya:

Verse opening
"Accha chalta hoon" [breath] "duaaon mein yaad rakhna" [breath] chord: G D C

Each phrase = one chord. Listen for the breath. Change chord at the breath. That's a huge chunk of the puzzle.

7Live singer's drill: accompany yourself

The most valuable drill in this course

"Sing & switch"

  1. Pick any song you can sing well from memory (Happy Birthday, a Bollywood verse, an English ballad — anything).
  2. Identify its key using Stage 1 methods.
  3. Play the I chord. Start singing the song.
  4. Hold the I chord as long as it sounds right. The moment it clashes, stop singing and try each of the family chords (IV, V, vi) silently in your head. Pick the one that "fits" — usually the one containing the note you're about to sing.
  5. Switch chord. Continue singing.
  6. Repeat. Make mistakes. The mistakes teach you the patterns.

First time: this will feel awkward and slow. After 50 attempts (about a week of daily practice), you'll start anticipating chord changes a beat before they happen. After 200 attempts, you'll do it without thinking — like talking.

9Common mistakes at this stage

Mistake 1

Changing chords too often. Beginners want to switch chords every half-second. Most songs hold one chord for 2–4 beats minimum. If your accompaniment feels frantic, hold each chord longer.

Mistake 2

Following the melody too literally. Not every melody note needs its own chord. Most melodies have a few "passing notes" between landing notes. Only the landing notes (where your voice rests for a beat) matter for chord choice.

Mistake 3

Ignoring the rhythm of phrases. A chord that fits the melody at one moment might be the wrong chord rhythmically because the song wants more movement. Trust the breath rule — change chords at phrase boundaries, not mid-phrase.

Mistake 4

Not using vi enough. Beginners get stuck on I, IV, V because those are the "happy three." But vi is what makes ballads emotional. If a song feels sad, try using vi much more than you think you should.

Mistake 5

Holding back vocally because you're afraid of being wrong. The whole point of this stage is that you have to sing out loud to develop the ear-to-hand connection. Sing badly, sing wrong notes, sing too quiet — but sing. Otherwise you're just reading theory.

10Mastery checklist for Stage 3

Ready for Stage 4 when you can:

  • Identify scale degrees (1-7) when you hear single notes against a I-chord drone, ~70% accuracy.
  • Sing a familiar song without backing and tell where the phrase-ending notes land (degree-wise).
  • Pick up your guitar, identify the key of a new song in <30 seconds, and play I along with the song.
  • Take a known melody and figure out the chord changes by ear within 2-3 listens.
  • Sing any song you know while comping yourself on guitar using I, IV, V, and vi — even if it's not the original chords, as long as it sounds musical.
  • Apply the 4-step live method without re-reading these notes.

11Preview of Stage 4

You can now find a key, name its chords, and pick chord changes that match a melody. The next stage is about movement — why certain chord sequences feel inevitable while others feel jarring. Cadences, the axis progression, modulation tricks, and the subtle art of substituting one chord for another to refresh a tired progression.
