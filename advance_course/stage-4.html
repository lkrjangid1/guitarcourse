Stage 04 · Movement

How chords move.

Until now you've focused on which chord to play. This stage is about where chords want to go next. Cadences, substitutions, key changes, and the subtle decisions that separate a beginner accompaniment from one that sounds professional.

0The motion principle

Music is movement. Chords don't sit still — they pull, push, resolve, suspend, and surprise. Every chord change creates a feeling, and the feeling depends on what came before.

Consider these two two-chord phrases in G major:

  • G → C: feels like "opening a door." Relaxing, expanding.
  • G → D: feels like "going somewhere." Forward motion.
  • G → Em: feels like "remembering something sad." Inward turn.
  • G → Am: feels like "stepping aside." Gentle detour.

Same starting chord. Four totally different emotional directions. This stage teaches you to choose the feeling.

"A great chord progression doesn't just play notes — it tells a story of leaving and returning home."

1The four cadences

A cadence is a short chord pattern that resolves (or fails to resolve) into the home chord. Think of it as the punctuation of music. The four cadences cover almost every "ending" you'll hear:

Cadence 1 · Authentic (the period)
V → I

The strongest possible resolution. Sounds like "the end." Used at the end of verses, choruses, songs. In a sentence: this is the full stop.

Example in G: D → G · feels conclusive, final, "home for sure."

Cadence 2 · Plagal (the amen)
IV → I

Gentler than V→I. Called the "amen cadence" because it's how hymns end. Less dramatic, more peaceful. Used when a song wants to end softly.

Example in G: C → G · feels peaceful, settled, like a sigh of relief.

Cadence 3 · Deceptive (the surprise)
V → vi

You hear V — you expect I to follow. Instead the song goes to vi. Feels like a sudden plot twist. Used to create surprise, often at the end of a verse to set up an unexpected chorus.

Example in G: D → Em · feels unresolved, melancholy, "this story isn't over."

Cadence 4 · Half (the question mark)
anything → V

Ends a phrase on V instead of resolving. Sounds like a question — unfinished, hanging. Used to lead into a new section (verse → chorus, etc.).

Example in G: ending on D after a sequence — feels suspended, waiting.

2The "axis" progression — the four-chord song

You met this in Stage 2 as I – V – vi – IV. Now let's see why it's so popular and how to play with it.

The axis is a perfect loop because each chord move feels "right":

  • I → V: creates tension
  • V → vi: deceptive cadence, surprise
  • vi → IV: peaceful descent
  • IV → I: plagal resolution, returns home

Every move feels natural. Every move creates expectation for the next. That's why thousands of songs use this exact loop — and why you've heard it a thousand times without noticing.

The four rotations

The axis has four "starting points," each with its own feel:

RotationPatternFeelExamples
StandardI – V – vi – IVAnthemic, optimisticLet It Be, Channa Mereya
Sadvi – IV – I – VReflective, romanticTum Hi Ho, Apologize
RisingIV – I – V – viHopeful, buildingOne Republic ballads
FallingV – IV – I – viResolution-then-melancholyMany emo ballads
Try this right now

Play all four rotations in G — strum each chord for 2 beats, loop them. Notice how the same four chords create radically different moods just by where they start. This is your playground.

3Chord substitutions — sounding fresh

Substitutions are the secret of musicians who make familiar progressions feel new. The idea: replace a chord with another chord that shares notes, but adds different color.

Common substitutions in any key

↔
vi
"Relative minor" — same family, sadder mood. Use when you want emotional weight on a chord that would normally feel bright.
IV
↔
ii
"Predominant pair" — both lead naturally to V. ii sounds more wistful, IV sounds more open.
V
↔
V7
"Add the 7th" — keeps the same root chord but adds extra pull toward I. Almost always an upgrade.
I
↔
Imaj7
"Dreamy version" — Gmaj7 instead of G. Softer, more contemplative. Great for ballads and ghazals.
V
↔
Vsus4 → V
"Suspension" — delays resolution. Play Dsus4 for a beat, then D, then I. Adds anticipation.

Substitution example: refreshing a tired progression

Original (axis): G — D — Em — C

Substituted version: Gmaj7 — D7 — Em7 — Cmaj7

Same progression. Instantly sounds more sophisticated. Try it.

4"Borrowed" chords from outside the key

Sometimes a song uses a chord that's not in the family. These are called borrowed chords — borrowed from the parallel minor (or major). They add color and surprise without changing the key.

The most common borrowed chord: ♭VII

In G major, the diatonic VII is F♯ diminished — almost never used. But the ♭VII (F major in G) is borrowed from G minor, and it sounds fantastic.

Try this progression: G — F — C — G (I — ♭VII — IV — I)

That F chord is "outside" the key, but feels totally at home. Used constantly in rock and folk — also a signature of Roop Tera Mastana and many Kishore-era classics.

The iv chord trick

In G major, the IV is C major. But if you play C minor instead — borrowed from G minor — it adds an instant "Bollywood ballad" feeling. Try: G — C — Cm — G. That move from C to Cm is gold.

5Modulation — changing keys mid-song

Modulation = changing keys partway through a song. Done well, it lifts the energy. Most commonly used in the last chorus to make it feel bigger.

The two easy modulations

1. Up by a half-step (the "key change")

G → A♭

Every chord shifts up one fret. Sounds dramatic, classic ballad ending move. Common in Whitney Houston, '90s pop, dramatic Bollywood reprises.

2. Up by a whole step

G → A

Every chord shifts up two frets. Even more dramatic. Common in showtune endings and inspirational anthems.

The "pivot chord" smooth modulation

Instead of an abrupt jump, you can pivot through a chord that exists in both keys. For example, modulating from G major to D major:

G major: G — D — Em — D (D is V in G major AND I in D major)
D major: D — G — A — D (continues smoothly)

The shared D chord acts as a hinge. The listener barely notices the key shift — it just feels like the song "opened up."

7The secondary dominant trick

This is the single coolest theory trick to add color to your playing: the secondary dominant.

The idea: any chord can have its own V that temporarily pulls toward it.

Standard example

In G major, ii is Am. The V of Am would be... E major (the V of A minor is E). But E is not in G major — it's borrowed from another key.

Watch this progression: G — E — Am — D — G

The E major chord (V of Am) creates a strong pull into the Am. Then Am — D — G resolves normally. The E feels exotic, surprising, professional. This is called "V of ii" or V/ii.

The cheat sheet
  • V/ii = a major chord on degree 6 (in G major: E). Pulls toward ii (Am).
  • V/IV = a major chord on degree 1, but with a ♭7 added — basically I7. In G: G7. Pulls toward IV (C).
  • V/V = a major chord on degree 2 (in G: A major). Pulls toward V (D).
  • V/vi = a major chord on degree 3 (in G: B major). Pulls toward vi (Em).

Hindi songs use these constantly. Notice that "extra" chord in Tum Hi Ho's pre-chorus that makes it feel cinematic? Often a secondary dominant.

8Bollywood-specific movement patterns

A few chord-movement patterns that show up everywhere in Hindi music:

The minor descent

Em — D — C — B7 (or Em — D — C — D in simpler versions)

The bass line walks down: E — D — C — B. Creates an aching, descending feeling. Used in countless ghazals and ballads.

The Bollywood loop

Am — F — C — G (vi — IV — I — V in C major)

The "sad axis" rotated to start on vi. Half of Hindi ballads use this exact loop. Try it under any sad Hindi melody — it'll fit.

The qawwali / sufi pulse

Dm — Gm — Dm — Am — Dm (i — iv — i — v — i in D minor)

Hypnotic, droning, all minor. The repeated i — iv — i creates a meditative quality. Used in sufi music, qawwali, ghazals.

9Recognising where you are in a song

Beyond knowing chords and progressions, professional players can predict what's coming based on song structure. Here's the structural map of a typical pop song:

SectionTypical progressionFunction
IntroOften I or vi-IV-I-VEstablishes the key
VerseOften I-V-vi-IV or vi-IV-I-VTells the story
Pre-chorusIV-V or ii-V, ending unresolvedBuilds tension
ChorusSame as verse or bigger versionReleases tension, hook
BridgeOften borrowed chords, modulates brieflySurprise / new emotional angle
Final chorusSometimes modulated upMaximum energy
OutroRepeats hook, often plagal cadenceWind down

Knowing these tendencies lets you anticipate: if you've heard one verse, you know what the second verse will likely do. You can prepare the chord change before the song demands it.

10Putting it together: Channa Mereya analysed

Let's apply Stage 4 thinking to one song you know well.

Key: G major (Stage 1)

Family: G, Am, Bm, C, D, Em (Stage 2)

Verse progression: G — C — G — C ... → I — IV loop

Just I and IV, slow. Creates a "wandering" feel. This is intentional — the song is reflective.

Chorus progression: G — D — Em — C ... → I — V — vi — IV (the axis)

Now the song "opens up." Adds V and vi, creating the full axis loop. The deceptive cadence V→vi mid-chorus (D→Em) gives that signature emotional twist.

Why it works:

  • Verse stays minimal (I-IV) so the chorus feels like a release.
  • The chorus axis pattern is universally satisfying.
  • The V→Em deceptive cadence creates the song's emotional core — "expected resolution, got melancholy instead."
  • Returns to I to feel resolved before looping.

11Mastery checklist for Stage 4

Ready for Stage 5 when you can:

  • Identify which cadence (authentic, plagal, deceptive, half) you're hearing at the end of a phrase, ~70% accuracy.
  • Play all four rotations of the axis (I-V-vi-IV and its rotations) in G, D, and Em from memory.
  • Substitute V with V7, I with Imaj7, IV with ii — in real time, without stopping.
  • Recognise when a song uses a borrowed chord (especially ♭VII or iv) and identify which one.
  • Analyse a familiar song and label its sections (verse, pre-chorus, chorus, bridge) by chord patterns alone.
  • Build an original 4- or 8-bar progression that has a clear "starts at home, leaves, returns home" arc.

12Preview of Stage 5

You've mastered which chords to play and where they're going. The next layer is rhythm — strumming, timing, and how to match the right rhythmic feel to the right song.

This isn't about memorising more patterns. It's about hearing the pulse of a song and choosing rhythm by mood: ballad, peppy folk, ghazal, sufi, party. By the end of Stage 5 you'll stop searching YouTube for "strumming pattern for X" because you'll feel the right pattern yourself.
