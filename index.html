Part 0

Foundations

1. Why learn guitar

The smartest way to learn guitar is to play things that are fun. Be the rockstar you always dreamed of.
Don't skip preparation. Everyone is different — but if you stay consistent, you will rock. Twenty minutes a day beats two hours once a week.

2. Course overview

Two parts. Sixteen days each. By the end you'll have four chord shapes, four strumming patterns, 31 practiced songs, and the foundation to play almost anything in any language.

4. Guitar action

Action = the gap between the strings and the fretboard. Too high and your fingers will hate you; too low and the strings buzz.

How to measure: tighten the 6th string, hold a small ruler at the 12th fret, and read off the gap.

ActionRangeVerdict
Low< 2.2 mm❌ Buzzy
Perfect2.2 – 2.8 mm✅ Sweet spot
High> 2.8 mm❌ Sore fingers
Most music shops will adjust action for free. If you're handy, a YouTube video plus an Allen key takes about 10 minutes.

5. The metronome

Greats like Sachin and Rahman played with perfect timing. Yours starts here.

A metronome helps you stay on the beat. Free apps work fine — GuitarTuna has one built in.

Two parameters to know:

  1. Beat count (time signature) — clicks per bar. 4/4 means 4 clicks per bar.
  2. Tempo — speed of those clicks, in BPM. 4/4 at 80 sounds slower than 4/4 at 120.

6. Tuning

Slow internet is frustrating. So is an untuned guitar.

Tuning sets the pitch of each open string by turning the tuning keys. Use a free app like GuitarTuna until your ears can do it themselves.

The mnemonic

"every Body Goes Dancing After Eating"

Strumming

Strumming is just downstroke (D) and upstroke (U) motion of your strumming hand. We'll learn the four most-used patterns in popular music.

The four core patterns

Pattern 1 — Downstrokes

1 bar = D D D D · The starter. Plain, even, on every beat.

Part 2 · Days 16–31

Chords

A chord is three or more notes played together. We'll learn just four — and with these four, you can play hundreds of songs in any language.

Meet the cast:

D   C   G   Em

Heads up. Your fingers will hurt for the next 15 days — there's no shortcut to building that callus. And: cut the nails on your chord-playing hand. Long nails make pressing strings impossible.

The four chord shapes

Chord 1 · D Major

Played with the 1st, 2nd & 3rd fingers.

  • 1st finger — 3rd string, 2nd fret
  • 2nd finger — 1st string, 2nd fret
  • 3rd finger — 2nd string, 3rd fret
D Major

Chord 2 · C Major

Played with the 1st, 2nd & 3rd fingers. You'll combine it with D in many songs.

  • 1st finger — 2nd string, 1st fret
  • 2nd finger — 4th string, 2nd fret
  • 3rd finger — 5th string, 3rd fret
C Major

Chord 3 · G Major

Played with the 1st, 2nd & 4th fingers. You'll use C and D alongside.

  • 1st finger — 5th string, 2nd fret
  • 2nd finger — 6th string, 3rd fret
  • 4th finger — 1st string, 3rd fret
G Major

Chord 4 · E Minor

The easiest chord on the guitar. Just 2nd & 3rd fingers.

  • 2nd finger — 5th string, 2nd fret
  • 3rd finger — 4th string, 2nd fret
E Minor

Course completion & post-work

Congratulations — you've reached the finish line. By now you can play hundreds of songs with the four chords and four strumming patterns you've internalised.

What's next

  • Polish — for the next week, replay every song in this book to perfection.
  • Add a fifth chord — A minor (Am). It opens dozens more songs.
  • Move to barre chords — covered in Part 5.
  • Study theory — Part 4 gives you a working understanding of why these chords go together.

Part 4 · Theory

Music Theory
(in Hindi notation)

The minimum theory you need to understand why the four chords work together — and to one day write your own songs. We'll use the Hindi sargam (सरगम) system throughout.

1. Notes & Sargam

In Hindi music, a note is called a स्वर (swar). There are seven natural notes (शुद्ध स्वर) and five altered ones (कोमल/तीव्र), making twelve in total — same as Western music, just named differently.

The seven natural sargam notes

Sa is moveable. Unlike fixed-pitch notation, in Indian music Sa is wherever you set your tonic. If you tune Sa to the open 5th string (A), the pitches shift accordingly.

2. The Sargam Clock

Arrange all 12 swaras on a circle. Move clockwise — pitch rises by a half-step (ardh-swar). Two steps clockwise = a whole step (poorn-swar).

3. The Major Scale (Bilawal Thaat)

The major scale uses all shuddha (natural) swaras. The interval pattern is:

whole · whole · half · whole · whole · whole · half

So from Sa:

सा — रे — ग — म — प — ध — नि — सां

Minor Scale (Asavari Thaat)

Use komal Ga, komal Dha, and komal Ni:

सा — रे — ग♭ — म — प — ध♭ — नि♭ — सां

5. Raag basics — Bhairav, Yaman, Bhairavi

A raag is more than a scale — it's a melodic personality. Each has rules about which swaras to emphasise (vadi, samvadi), forbidden movements (varjit), characteristic phrases (pakad), and the time of day or season it suits.

Raag Yaman (राग यमन)

  • Thaat: Kalyan
  • Swaras: सा रे ग म' प ध नि सां (tivra Madhyam — म')
  • Time: Early evening (sandhi-prakash)
  • Vadi: Ga · Samvadi: Ni
  • Pakad (signature phrase): नि रे ग म' प, म' ग रे सा
  • Mood: Romantic, peaceful, expansive
  • Famous Bollywood: Jiya Le Gayi Ji (Anpadh), Eri Sakhi Mangal Gao

Raag Bhairav (राग भैरव)

  • Thaat: Bhairav
  • Swaras: सा रे♭ ग म प ध♭ नि सां (komal Re & Dha)
  • Time: Early morning (just before sunrise)
  • Vadi: Dha · Samvadi: Re
  • Pakad: ग म ध♭ प, ग म रे♭ सा
  • Mood: Devotional, serene, weighty
  • Famous Bollywood: Mohe Bhool Gaye Saawariya (Baiju Bawra), Jago Mohan Pyare

Raag Bhairavi (राग भैरवी)

  • Thaat: Bhairavi
  • Swaras: सा रे♭ ग♭ म प ध♭ नि♭ सां (all five komal possibilities)
  • Time: Late morning, but traditionally played to conclude any concert regardless of time
  • Vadi: Ma · Samvadi: Sa
  • Pakad: म ग♭ रे♭ सा, नि♭ ध♭ प
  • Mood: Bittersweet, devotional, deeply moving
  • Famous Bollywood: Aaj Sajan Mohe Ang Laga Lo (Pyaasa), Tu Ganga Ki Mauj
Why this matters for guitarists: Bollywood songwriters lean heavily on raag colour. Knowing Yaman lets you instantly hear why Channa Mereya feels nostalgic; knowing Bhairavi explains why Tum Hi Ho feels bittersweet.

9. The Circle of Fifths (Hindi-style)

Move clockwise, you add one tivra (sharp). Counter-clockwise, you add one komal (flat). Neighbours on the circle share most of their swaras — useful for picking a key that's easy to modulate to.

9. Genre-specific lessons

Bollywood

Lean on the Em–D–C–G or Am–F–C–G loop. Add a fingerpicked arpeggio for ballads, the pop-strum for upbeat numbers. Many Bollywood songs sit in raag Yaman or Kafi — colour them by emphasising the relevant swaras during fills.

Ghazal

Almost always in 3/4 or slow 4/4 with sparse strumming. Use sus2 and sus4 chords to create the floating, contemplative feel. Em–Am–C–B7 (B7 instead of D) brings the classic ghazal tension.

Sufi

Drone-heavy. Pick a tonic chord (often Em) and hold it while the melody travels. Add a capo to match the qawwali's pitch. Listen to Tu Maane Ya Na Maane, Allah Hoo.

Rock

Power chords + palm muting + distortion. Riff first, lyrics second. Five-chord vocabulary: E5, A5, D5, G5, C5. Try "Smoke on the Water" or "Seven Nation Army" as your first rock song.

Blues

The 12-bar blues form is your sandbox. In E: E E E E A A E E B A E B. Use the E minor pentatonic + blue note. Bend the 3rd and 5th. Listen to BB King.

Country

D-U-D-U all day. Travis-pick verses, strum choruses. Heavy use of G, C, D, Em — your existing vocabulary. Listen to Wagon Wheel, Take Me Home Country Roads.

Classical (Spanish/fingerstyle)

Nylon strings, no pick. PIMA right-hand, all four left-hand fingers. Play Romance, Cavatina, or arpeggiated Bach.

Part 6 · Practice & Ear

Practice & ear training

Skill comes from focused, consistent practice — not from how much time you spend with the instrument in your lap. Pick one of these routines and stick to it.

1. Practice routines

30-minute routine — busy days
  • 5 min — warmup: chromatic exercise, finger stretches
  • 10 min — chord changes (G→C, G→D, C→D) to a metronome
  • 10 min — one song, focused
  • 5 min — strumming pattern drill
60-minute routine — your default
  • 5 min — warmup
  • 10 min — scales (one mode + pentatonic)
  • 15 min — chord changes / barre chord drill
  • 20 min — two songs
  • 5 min — ear training (interval recognition)
  • 5 min — free play / improvisation
90-minute routine — weekends

Take the 60-minute routine and add:

  • +15 min — technique work (bends, slides, fingerstyle)
  • +15 min — theory study or learning a new song from scratch
The golden rule: slow & clean beats fast & sloppy every single time. If you can't play it slowly, you can't play it fast.

2. Ear training

Your ear is more important than your hands. Spend five minutes a day on this and within months you'll start to hear songs and instinctively know what chord is playing.

Daily 5-minute drill (sargam-based)

  1. Sing the natural sargam — सा रे ग म प ध नि सां, slowly, in tune.
  2. Sing intervals — from Sa, jump to Ga (third), Pa (fifth), Sa' (octave). Match against your guitar.
  3. Identify chord quality — major or minor? Have a friend (or a YouTube video) play random chords; guess the type before they reveal.
  4. Match pitch — pluck a string, sing it back, then check with a tuner.

Songs by ear — start simple

  • Happy Birthday
  • Twinkle Twinkle (in sargam: सा सा प प ध ध प · म म ग ग रे रे सा)
  • Sare Jahaan Se Achchha
  • Jingle Bells

Find each melody on one string, by trial and error. This builds relative pitch — the practical skill of hearing what's coming next.

Apps

  • Functional Ear Trainer — best free interval app
  • EarMaster — comprehensive, paid
  • Tenuto — clean, iOS
  • Toned Ear — browser-based, free

3. Reading chord charts & tabs

Chord diagram cheat-sheet

  • Numbers above strings = which finger to use (1=index, 2=middle, 3=ring, 4=pinky)
  • × above a string = don't play that string
  • ○ above a string = play it open (no finger)
  • Top horizontal bar = the nut (top of the fretboard)

Tab basics

e |--0--3--5--|     ← thinnest string (high E)
B |--1--3--5--|
G |--0--0--5--|
D |--2--0--5--|
A |--3--2--5--|
E |--x--3--5--|     ← thickest string (low E)
   ↑
   x = mute / don't play

Each line is a string (top = high). Numbers = fret to press. Vertical alignment means play together (chord). Left to right = time.

Part 7 · Recording

Home recording basics

Once you can play a song cleanly, the next leap is hearing yourself back. It exposes timing problems, weak chord changes, and tone issues you'd never notice in the moment.

1. Minimum gear

  • A laptop (any modern one)

  • For electric: a quarter-inch cable straight into the interface (no mic needed)
  • Closed-back headphones — never use speakers (they cause feedback when monitoring)
  • A DAW (Digital Audio Workstation) — free options below

6. Tracking tips

  • Always record to a click track — it makes editing a thousand times easier.
  • Multiple takes — record three full passes, pick the best.
  • Double-track rhythm guitars — record the same part twice, pan one left and one right. Instant thickness.
  • Tune between every take — strings detune surprisingly fast.
  • Sing while you play if you'll add vocals later — your timing improves dramatically.

7. Studio session etiquette

One day someone will book you for a session. Showing up with skill is half the job; showing up like a professional is the other half.

Before the session
  • Arrive 15 minutes early. Set up quietly while the engineer dials your sound.
  • Bring fresh strings (changed within 48 hours), spare picks, capo, slide, tuner, and a backup cable.
  • Listen to the demo on the way over. Don't show up cold.
  • Email any chord charts in advance — engineers love this.
During the session
  • Play to the click. Always. Even when it feels stiff — the producer can swing it later.
  • Mistakes are fine; recovering quickly is the skill. Don't apologise for two minutes after a fluff.
  • The producer's word is final, even if you disagree musically.
  • Don't noodle between takes. Mute the strings or rest the guitar. Engineer ears need the silence.
  • Phones on silent. No exceptions.
After
  • Pack quickly. Studios are billed by the hour.
  • Send a polite thank-you. The next call usually comes from the engineer, not the artist.
Part 8 · Beyond

The long game

You've finished the 31 days. Here's everything that helps you keep growing — common pitfalls, beginner shortcuts, gear advice, maintenance, and a 90-day chart to keep you accountable.

1. Common mistakes (and how to fix them)

Buzzing strings

The most common beginner sound. Three causes: (1) finger not pressing hard enough, (2) finger placed too far from the fret wire (always press just behind the wire, not in the middle), or (3) action too low. Fix by repositioning your finger first; if every chord buzzes, get the action checked.

Weak chord transitions

You can play each chord cleanly, but switching between them creates a half-second pause. Fix: practice just the transition. Two chords, slowly, no strumming, just lift-and-place. 20 reps a day for a week kills this problem.

The "death grip"

Squeezing the neck like you're choking it. Causes pain, bad tone, and slow speed. Fix: thumb behind the neck, light pressure. Try playing a chord, then relax your hand just until the strings start to buzz, then add the tiniest amount back. That's correct pressure.

Ignoring the 4th finger (pinky)

Beginners use index, middle, and ring — and pretend the pinky doesn't exist. This caps your ceiling permanently. Fix: do daily chromatic exercises (1-2-3-4 on each string, every fret) to build pinky strength. Within two weeks it's no longer the weak link.

Practising only what you already know

Comfortable practice isn't real practice. If everything you played today felt easy, you didn't progress. Aim to spend half your practice on things you can't quite play yet.

2. Two-finger and one-finger song hacks

Day 1, before you've built any callus or memorised any shape, you can already play real songs.

One-finger songs (just Em — 2 fingers, but easy)

Em uses two fingers; nothing else. Songs that work with just Em:

  • Pyaar Hua Ikraar Hua (v1)
  • Hare Krishna Hare Raam chant
  • Any one-chord drone-style sufi piece

Two-chord songs (Em + D)

  • Zindagi Ek Safar (mostly Em + D)
  • Eleanor Rigby — Beatles (Em + C, but Em + D works as a starter)
  • Horse with No Name — America (Em + D6add9, simplifies to Em + D)

Three-chord songs (Em + D + C)

Add C and you unlock dozens more — most folk songs, most camp-fire singalongs, most early Beatles.

The 4-chord cheat: Em → C → G → D played as a loop fits an embarrassing percentage of pop songs ever written. Loop it on a backing track and try singing any pop song you know — you'll be shocked how many fit.

3. Maintenance

Changing strings (step-by-step)

  1. Loosen the old string by turning the tuning peg until it's slack.
  2. Cut with wire cutters near the soundhole, pull through the bridge pin (acoustic) or tailpiece (electric).
  3. Pop the bridge pin out with a string winder (or pliers — gently).
  4. Insert new string ball-end first into the bridge hole. Push the bridge pin back in firmly while pulling slack out of the string.
  5. Thread through the tuning post, leaving about 2 inches of slack.
  6. Wind, making sure each new wrap goes below the previous one (this locks the string).
  7. Tune up, then stretch each string by pulling it gently away from the fretboard a few times. Re-tune.
  8. Strings will go out of tune for the first day — this is normal. Re-tune frequently.

Humidity

Wood guitars hate humidity extremes. Ideal range: 45–55%. Below 35% and the wood cracks; above 65% and the neck warps. In dry winters, use a soundhole humidifier (Oasis, D'Addario Humidipak) — ₹1,500 buys one that lasts years.

Neck adjustment (truss rod)

If your action goes up over time and the neck looks bowed, the truss rod needs adjustment. Don't DIY this without a tutorial — over-tightening can crack the neck. Most music shops do it for ₹200–500.

Intonation

Your guitar is "intonated" when fretted notes match open notes at the 12th fret. If a chord at fret 7 sounds out of tune even though your open strings are perfect, intonation is off. Fix: at the bridge, lengthen or shorten the saddle for that string. Again, most shops do this in 10 minutes.

Routine care

  • Wipe down strings with a soft cloth after every session — extends string life dramatically.
  • Polish the body with a dedicated guitar polish every few months. Never use furniture polish.
  • Store in a case (not on a stand) if humidity in your area swings hard.
  • Loosen strings a quarter-turn each if storing for more than a month.
